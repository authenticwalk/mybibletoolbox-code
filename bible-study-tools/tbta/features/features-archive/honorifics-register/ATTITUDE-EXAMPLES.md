# Speaker Demographics: Verse Examples

This document provides concrete verse examples showing how the 6 speaker demographics features work together. Each example includes the verse, feature values, reasoning, and translation implications.

---

## Example Set 1: Honorable Attitude (Authority Figures)

### Example 1.1: God Speaking to Moses (Exodus 3:5)

**Verse**: "Then he said, 'Do not come near; take your sandals off your feet, for the place on which you are standing is holy ground.'"

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: M (God)
- Listener: S (Moses)
- Speaker's Attitude: H (Honorable)
- Speaker's Age: N (Not Applicable - Divine)
- Speaker-Listener Age: N (Not Applicable - Divine to human)
- Speech Style: Formal

**Reasoning**:
- God is speaking from the burning bush, establishing authority
- The command is direct but carries divine weight
- Context is sacred revelation, requiring formal register
- Even though God is commanding (which could be seen as superior to subordinate), the divine nature calls for "Honorable" marking

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Use sonkeigo (尊敬語) - respectful language, but God's speech about Moses requires keigo. Moses addressed with respectful forms.
- **Korean**: Use highest formal endings (-습니다), with honorific particles
- **Thai**: Use royal/sacred register with formal particles (ครับ/คะ)
- **Javanese**: Use krama inggil (highest register)

---

### Example 1.2: Jesus Teaching the Crowd (Matthew 5:3)

**Verse**: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: R (Jesus)
- Listener: C (Crowd)
- Speaker's Attitude: H (Honorable)
- Speaker's Age: C (Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: S (Same Age - mixed crowd)
- Speech Style: Didactic (Teaching style)

**Reasoning**:
- Jesus is teaching authoritatively as Rabbi/Teacher
- The Sermon on the Mount is formal public teaching
- "Honorable" reflects Jesus's authoritative teaching role
- Speech Style "Didactic" indicates this is instruction, not casual conversation

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Use teineigo (丁寧語) formal polite forms with -masu/-desu
- **Korean**: Formal endings but not highest honorific (교사 register)
- **Hindi**: Formal pronouns (आप) and respectful verb forms
- **Vietnamese**: Formal pronouns (thầy for teacher speaking)

---

### Example 1.3: Paul's Apostolic Authority (1 Corinthians 1:10)

**Verse**: "I appeal to you, brothers, by the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that all of you agree..."

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: P (Paul)
- Listener: C (Corinthian church)
- Speaker's Attitude: H (Honorable)
- Speaker's Age: C (Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: S (Same Age/Peer)
- Speech Style: Formal

**Reasoning**:
- Paul writes with apostolic authority despite calling them "brothers"
- The appeal "by the name of our Lord" elevates the register
- This is formal correspondence requiring respect
- Even peer-age relationships can have "Honorable" attitude in authoritative contexts

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Mix of teineigo (polite) with respectful address (兄弟たち)
- **Korean**: Formal register with respectful but not highest forms
- **Indonesian**: Use Saudara-saudara (formal address) with complete sentences

## Example Set 2: Neutral Attitude (Standard Respectful)

### Example 2.1: Jesus and Nicodemus (John 3:3)

**Verse**: "Jesus answered him, 'Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God.'"

- Speaker: R (Jesus)
- Listener: N (Nicodemus - Pharisee)
- Speaker's Attitude: n (Neutral)
- Speaker's Age: C (Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: S (Same Age - both adults)
- Speech Style: Formal

**Reasoning**:
- This is a serious theological conversation between adults
- Jesus treats Nicodemus respectfully but not with excessive deference
- "Neutral" indicates standard respectful dialogue, not intimate nor highly formal
- The teaching context maintains formality

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Standard teineigo (polite forms), not casual
- **Korean**: Standard formal endings, neutral politeness
- **Mandarin**: Formal pronouns (您) with respectful tone
- **Thai**: Standard polite particles (ครับ/คะ)

## Example Set 3: Familiar Attitude (Close Relationships)

### Example 3.1: Jesus with Disciples at Last Supper (John 13:33)

**Verse**: "Little children, yet a little while I am with you."

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: R (Jesus)
- Listener: D (Disciples)
- Speaker's Attitude: F (Familiar)
- Speaker's Age: C (Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: S (Same Age/Peers)
- Speech Style: Informal

**Reasoning**:
- Jesus uses affectionate address "Little children" (τεκνία)
- This is intimate moment at Last Supper, not public teaching
- "Familiar" reflects close relationship after 3 years together
- Informal style appropriate for private gathering

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Casual forms acceptable here (kudaketa kotoba), possibly without keigo
- **Korean**: Banmal (casual speech) or intimate formal (해요 체)
- **Thai**: Can drop some formal particles in intimate settings
- **Vietnamese**: Intimate pronouns (em for endearment)

- Speaker's Age: B (Young Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: S (Same Age - peers)

## Example Set 4: Endearing Attitude (Intimate/Affectionate)

### Example 4.1: Ruth to Naomi (Ruth 1:16)

**Verse**: "But Ruth said, 'Do not urge me to leave you or to return from following you. For where you go I will go...'"

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: R (Ruth)
- Listener: n (Naomi)
- Speaker's Attitude: E (Endearing)
- Speaker's Age: B (Young Adult)
- Speaker-Listener Age: Y (Speaker younger, significant)
- Speech Style: Informal

**Reasoning**:
- Ruth speaks to mother-in-law with deep affection
- Despite age difference (Ruth younger), relationship is intimate
- "Endearing" reflects covenant-like devotion
- Age relationship "Y" (speaker younger significant) important for languages needing deference

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Mix of respectful (for elder) but warm/affectionate particles (ne, wa for female speech)
- **Korean**: Respectful jondaemal (존댓말) but with warm tone (어머니 for mother-in-law)
- **Thai**: Elder respect particles (คะ) but warm affectionate vocabulary
- **Javanese**: Madya or krama with affectionate lexical choices

## Example Set 6: Age-Relationship Focus

### Example 6.1: Elder to Younger - Timothy Receiving Instruction (2 Timothy 1:2)

**Verse**: "To Timothy, my beloved child: Grace, mercy, and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Lord."

**Feature Values**:
- Speaker: P (Paul)
- Listener: t (Timothy)
- Speaker's Attitude: E (Endearing)
- Speaker's Age: D (Elder)
- Speaker-Listener Age: O (Speaker Older, significant - 20+ years)
- Speech Style: Informal

**Reasoning**:
- Paul as spiritual father to Timothy (significant age gap)
- "My beloved child" (ἀγαπητῷ τέκνῳ) shows affection
- Age relationship "O" (speaker older significant) crucial for languages with age-based speech
- Despite age difference, "Endearing" attitude shows warmth

**Translation Implications**:
- **Japanese**: Elder can use casual forms to younger, but with affection
- **Korean**: Elder speaking down but warmly (가 form or intimate)
- **Vietnamese**: Use appropriate pronouns (ông for elder speaker, con for younger listener)
- **Thai**: Elder addressing younger with appropriate particles
- **Javanese**: Can use ngoko (familiar) from elder to younger

## Summary of Feature Interactions

### High-Frequency Combinations

1. **Speaker=M (God) + Attitude=H + Style=Formal/Liturgical**
   - God speaking requires highest respect markers
   - Languages: All languages with honorifics

2. **Speaker=R (Jesus) + Listener=D (Disciples) + Attitude=n/F + Age=S**
   - Teaching disciples uses neutral or familiar speech
   - Languages: Japanese, Korean (can modulate formality)

3. **Age=Y (Younger) + Attitude=H + Style=Formal**
   - Younger addressing elder requires respect
   - Languages: Japanese, Korean, Thai, Vietnamese, Javanese, Indonesian

4. **Prayer contexts: Listener=M (God) + Attitude=H + Style=Liturgical**
   - Universal pattern across all cultures
   - Languages: All languages have special prayer registers

### Validation Patterns

When reviewing TBTA annotations:
1. **Divine speech**: Always check if attitude and style are appropriate
2. **Age relationships**: Verify younger speakers show appropriate respect
3. **Style consistency**: Liturgical style should only appear in prayer/worship
4. **Peer speech**: Same-age peers can vary from Familiar to Neutral
5. **Authority figures**: Teachers, leaders typically use Honorable or Neutral

## Language-Specific Notes

### Japanese
- Most sensitive to all 6 features
- Keigo system (sonkeigo, kenjougo, teineigo) maps directly to Attitude values
- Age relationship critical for particle selection
- Speech style affects verb endings and vocabulary

### Korean
- Grammatically obligatory marking (banmal vs jondaemal)
- Age difference as small as 1 year affects register
- Attitude determines verb ending patterns
- Speaker's Age and Age Relationship most critical

### Thai
- Final particles differ by gender (ครับ vs คะ/ค่ะ)
- Royal/sacred language for divine speech
- Age-based pronouns essential
- Monastic contexts have special registers

### Javanese
- 10+ distinct speech levels
- Age and social status both determine register
- Ngoko (low) vs krama (mid) vs krama inggil (high)
- Most complex honorific system in TBTA languages

### Vietnamese
- Pronoun system encodes all relationships
- Age difference determines pronoun selection
- Family terms used for non-family (respect)
- Speaker-Listener Age most critical feature
