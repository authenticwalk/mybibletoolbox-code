## Major Families

### 1. Otomanguean (69 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Amuzgo (Guerrero, San Pedro Amuzgos), Chatino (Nopala, Tataltepec, Western Highland), Chinantec (Comaltepec, Lalana, Lealao, Ozumacín, Palantla, Quiotepec, Sochiapam, Tepetotutla, Usila), Cuicatec (Tepeuxila, Teutila), Mazahua (Central), Mazatec (Ayautla, Chiquihuitlán, Huautla, Jalapa de Díaz, San Jerónimo Tecóatl), Mixtec (Atatlahuca, Chayuco, Coatzospan, Diuxi-Tilantongo, Jamiltepec, Magdalena Peñasco, Ocotepec, Peñoles, Pinotepa Nacional, San Juan Colorado, San Miguel el Grande, Silacayoapan, Southern Puebla, Tezoatlán, Yosondúa), Otomi (Eastern Highland, Mezquital, Querétaro, Tenango, Estado de México), Popoloca (San Juan Atzingo, San Marcos Tlacoyalco), Triqui (Copala), Zapotec (Amatlán, Cajonos, Chichicapan, Choapan, Coatecas Altas, Isthmus, Lachixío, Miahuatlán, Mitla, Mixtepec, Ocotlán, Ozolotepec, Quioquitani-Quierí, Rincón, Santo Domingo Albarradas, Sierra de Juárez, Southern Rincon, Tabaa, Texmelucan, Western Tlacolula Valley, Yalálag, Yatee, Yatzachi, Zoogocho)

**Geographic distribution**: Mesoamerica, primarily Mexico (Oaxaca, Puebla, Guerrero, Veracruz states)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Tone Systems** (Source: Wikipedia - Oto-Manguean languages)
   - ALL Oto-Manguean languages are tonal - oldest language family with evidence of tonal contrast in proto-language
   - Three-tone systems most common (Popolocan, Tlapanec, Amuzgo, Mixtecan, Zapotecan, Chinantecan)
   - Two-tone systems in Oto-Pamean
   - Complex systems: Usila Chinantec has FIVE level tones
   - Tone distinguishes both root meanings AND grammatical categories
   - Tone used extensively for inflection

2. **Aspect-Oriented Verbal System** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Aspect more relevant than tense
   - Valency and aspect (transitive, intransitive, causative, inchoative, iterative, imperfective, perfective) marked by prefixes/proclitics
   - Person and number of subject/object expressed by suffixes/clitics

3. **Word Order** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Head-initial structure
   - Predominant word order: Verb-Subject-Object (VSO)

4. **Noun Classifiers** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Some languages have classifiers marking: human, familiar person, animate, house, fruit

5. **Inflectional Complexity** (Source: Compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com)
   - Due to large tone inventory used for inflection, these languages have some of the most complex inflection systems in the world

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Low-resource technology challenge**: Models drastically underperform for Oto-Manguean languages due to complex inflection in stems, affixes, AND tonal changes all present in one word (Source: Multilingual-technologies.netlify.app)
- **Tonal accuracy critical**: Tone changes affect both lexical meaning and grammatical function
- **Aspect vs. tense**: Translators must recognize aspect-based rather than tense-based temporal systems
- **Complex morphology**: Single words may encode multiple grammatical categories through tone, affixes, and stem changes

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Oto-Manguean languages
- Compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com: "Otomanguean historical linguistics: Past, present, and prospects for the future"
- Academia.edu: "Inflectional class complexity in the Oto-Manguean languages"
- Multilingual-technologies.netlify.app: "Morphological Inflection for Oto-Manguean Languages"

### 2. Mayan (41 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Achi, Akateko, Awakateko, Chol, Chontal (Tabasco), Chuj, Ch'orti', Huastec, Ixil, Jakalteko, Kaqchikel, K'iche', Lacandon, Mam, Maya (Mopán), Poqomchi', Q'eqchi', Tektiteko, Tojolabal, Tzotzil, Tz'utujil, Uspanteko

**Geographic distribution**: Mesoamerica (Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Ergativity** (Source: Wikipedia - Mayan languages)
   - Morphologically ergative alignment
   - Subject of intransitive verb treated similarly to object of transitive verb
   - Two sets of affixes:
     - Set B: subjects of intransitive verbs + objects of transitive verbs
     - Set A: subjects of transitive verbs
   - Syntactic ergativity: constraints on extraction of transitive subjects for focus/interrogation/negation/relativization

2. **Polysynthetic Verb Complex** (Source: MustGo.com)
   - Uses both prefixes and suffixes for grammatical function
   - Affixes signal aspect, tense, mood
   - Directional clitics placed after aspect/absolutive markers
   - Position varies: before verb (intransitive) or before ergative marker (transitive)

3. **Rich Voice System** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Proto-Mayan had at least one passive construction
   - Antipassive rule for downplaying agent relative to patient
   - Multiple voice-changing operations available

4. **Word Order** (Source: Bilinguistics.com)
   - Verb-initial: VOS or VSO
   - Contrasts sharply with English SVO

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Ergative alignment**: Requires different conceptualization of subject/object relationships than accusative languages
- **Polysynthesis**: Single verb can express what requires multiple words in English
- **Extraction constraints**: Syntactic ergativity affects question formation, focus constructions, relative clauses
- **Directional system**: Grammaticalized directional meanings require careful interpretation

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Mayan languages, Classic Maya language
- MustGo.com: Mayan Language Family
- Bilinguistics.com: "Mayan Speech and Language Development"
- Frontiers.org: "The Acquisition of Directionals in Two Mayan Languages"

### 3. Australian (36 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Alyawarr, Anindilyakwa, Anmatyerre, Arrarnta (Western), Arrernte (Eastern), Awabakal, Burarra, Dhangu-Djangu, Dhuwaya, Diyari, Djambarrpuyngu, Djinang, Garrwa, Gumatj, Gunwinggu, Kala Lagaw Ya, Kuku-Yalanji, Kunjen, Kuuk Thayorre, Martu Wangka, Maung, Murrinh-Patha, Ngaanyatjarra, Ngarrindjeri, Nunggubuyu, Nyangumarta, Nyungar, Pintupi-Luritja, Pitjantjatjara, Tiwi, Walmajarri, Warlpiri, Wik-Mungkan, Worrorra, Yanyuwa

**Geographic distribution**: Australia

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Case Systems** (Source: Wikipedia - Australian Aboriginal languages)
   - Many languages have ergative-absolutive case systems
   - Split ergativity patterns common:
     - Pronouns (1st/2nd person) use nominative-accusative
     - 3rd person uses ergative-absolutive
     - Alternative split: animate vs. inanimate

2. **Free Word Order** (Source: Britannica)
   - Many languages feature free word order
   - Contrasts dramatically with English syntactic ordering
   - Syntactic coherence created by:
     - Verb inflection
     - Noun case marking
   - NOT by word order

3. **Kinship-Influenced Grammar** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Kinship relations shape grammar in ways seen nowhere else
   - Dual pronouns (referring to two persons) have distinct forms based on relationship between referents
   - Example from Warlpiri: triangular terms indicate relation of both speaker and listener to referent
     - "my mother who is also your father's sister"

4. **Avoidance Speech Registers** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Special speech registers used only in presence of certain close relatives
   - Share phonology and grammar with standard language
   - Different and restricted lexicon

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Kinship untranslatability**: English "uncle" has no exact equivalent; Western Desert uses different words for mother's brother vs. father's brother
- **Free word order**: Cannot rely on position to determine grammatical relations
- **Case marking critical**: Must attend to case suffixes, not word order
- **Split ergativity**: Treatment of arguments varies by person/animacy hierarchy
- **Avoidance registers**: Some texts may use specialized vocabulary requiring cultural knowledge

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Australian Aboriginal languages, Australian Aboriginal kinship
- Britannica: "Australian Aboriginal languages - Dialects, Classification, Diversity"
- MustGo.com: "Indigenous Languages of Australia"
- Pacific-credo Publications: "Australian Aboriginal Kinship - Part two"

### 4. Afro-Asiatic (25 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Arabic (Standard), Assyrian Neo-Aramaic, Coptic, Dangaléat, Dawro, Gamo, Gofa, Hamer-Banna, Hausa, Hdi, Hebrew, Hebrew (Ancient), Male, Mbuko, Merey, Muyang, Somali, Wolaytta

**Geographic distribution**: North Africa, Horn of Africa, Middle East (primarily Semitic, Chadic, Cushitic branches represented)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Root-and-Pattern Morphology** (Source: Britannica)
   - Basic word meaning manifested in consonants alone
   - Vowel sequence (pattern) adds grammatical information
   - May combine with prefixes/suffixes
   - Classic Arabic example: K-T-B root (writing)
     - KiTaB = book
     - KaTaBa = to write
     - KaTtaBa = to make write (teach)
     - maKTaB = office
     - KaaTiB = writer
     - maKTaBa = library
     - miKTaB = typewriter
     - KuTuBii = bookseller
     - maKTuuB = fate ("that which is written")

2. **Consonant and Vowel Lengthening** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Gemination (consonant lengthening) common throughout family
   - Vowel lengthening (double vowels) also common

3. **Grammatical Gender** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Most languages use masculine/feminine gender
   - Exceptions exist (some Central Chadic languages)

4. **Tonal Distribution** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Majority of languages are tonal: Omotic, Chadic, Cushitic
   - Tone ABSENT in Berber and Semitic branches

5. **Universal Categories** (Source: MustGo.com)
   - All have: verb, noun, adposition (prepositions/postpositions)

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Consonantal roots**: Root meaning preserved across derivations; vowel patterns modify meaning
- **Non-linear morphology**: Cannot simply segment words linearly; must recognize root-pattern interdigitation
- **Tonal variation**: Some branches tonal, others not - affects transliteration and phonological analysis
- **Gender agreement**: Extensive gender concord throughout noun phrases and verb agreement

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Afroasiatic languages
- Britannica: "Afro-Asiatic languages - Morphology, Semitic, Berber"
- MustGo.com: "Afro Asiatic Language Family"
- Knowledge.Deck.no: "Afro-Asiatic Languages"

### 5. Uto-Aztecan (21 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Cora (El Nayar), Hopi, Huichol, Nahuatl (Central Huasteca, Eastern Huasteca, Guerrero, Highland Puebla, Michoacán, Northern Oaxaca, Northern Puebla, Southeastern Puebla, Tetelcingo, Western Huasteca, Zacatlán-Ahuacatlán-Tepetzintla), Paiute (Northern), Tarahumara (Western), Tepehuan (Northern, Southeastern), Tohono O'odham, Yaqui

**Geographic distribution**: Southwestern United States and Mexico (from Oregon to Panama historically, concentrated in Mexico)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Polysynthetic Structure** (Source: EBSCO Research Starters)
   - Verbs carry multiple grammatical functions within single word
   - Complex verb constructions can form complete sentences independently
   - Functions blended into verb via suffixes, prefixes, infixes, vowel variation:
     - Subjective pronouns
     - Objective pronouns
     - Reflexive pronouns
     - Tense markers
     - Voice markers
     - Mode markers
     - Direction markers

2. **Incorporation** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Highly incorporative languages
   - Multiple affixes added to roots form very long words

3. **Word Order** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Verbs placed after subjects and objects in clauses (SOV tendency)

4. **Classical Nahuatl Example** (Source: EBSCO)
   - "onikpix" = complete sentence "I held it"
     - o- = past tense marker
     - ni- = "I"
     - k- = "it"
     - pix = "to have/hold"

5. **Development of Polysynthesis** (Source: Academia.edu)
   - Multiple non-polysynthetic Uto-Aztecan languages display properties attributed to polysynthesis
   - Nahuatl polysynthesis developed in discrete series of stages
   - Each stage has attestation elsewhere in family

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Verb-centered syntax**: Single verb word can express entire proposition
- **Multiple arguments**: Verbs can incorporate subject, object, direction, tense, aspect, mode
- **Pronoun incorporation**: Separate pronoun words not always required
- **Complex morpheme ordering**: Strict ordering of affixes within verb complex

**Sources consulted**:
- EBSCO Research Starters: "Uto-Aztecan Language Family"
- Wikipedia: Uto-Aztecan languages
- MustGo.com: "Uto-Aztecan Language Family - Origins & Classification"
- Academia.edu: "On the Gradual Development of Polysynthesis in Nahuatl"

## Medium Families

### 6. Maipurean / Arawakan (20 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Ajyíninka Apurucayali, Apurinã, Asháninka, Ashéninka (Pajonal, Pichis, South Ucayali, Ucayali-Yurúa), Caquinte, Garifuna, Ignaciano, Matsigenka, Nomatsigenga, Palikúr, Parecís, Piapoco, Terêna, Wapishana, Yanesha', Yine, Yucuna

**Geographic distribution**: South America (Amazon basin, Orinoco, Caribbean)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Morphological Structure** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Synthetic, predominantly head-marking, suffixing
   - Closed and historically stable set of prefixes:
     - Bound pronouns on verbs
     - Relativizing prefix ka-
     - Negative counterpart ma-

2. **Person Marking** (Source: ScienceDirect)
   - Prefixes mark:
     - Subject of transitive verb
     - Subject of intransitive active verb
     - Possessor on nouns
   - Suffixes/enclitics express (in 2/3 of languages):
     - Object of transitive verb (O)
     - Subject of stative verbs (So)
     - Subject of non-verbal predicates

3. **Agreement Prefixes** (Source: ScienceDirect)
   - Proto-forms reconstructed:
     - *nu- = 1SG
     - *pi- = 2SG
     - *ri- = 3SG MASC
     - *tu- = 3SG FEM
     - *wa- = 1PL
     - *xi- = 2PL
     - *na- = 3PL

4. **Noun Classification** (Source: Oxford Research Encyclopedia)
   - More than half the languages have complex classifier systems
   - Classifiers on:
     - Number words
     - Verbs
     - Possessive constructions
     - Nouns themselves
   - Categorize nouns by: shape, consistency, animacy

5. **Gender Distinction** (Source: Oxford Research Encyclopedia)
   - Most languages distinguish masculine/feminine in:
     - 3rd person singular pronouns
     - Demonstratives
     - Nominalizations
     - Adjective agreement markers

6. **Possession** (Source: SIL.org)
   - If possessor not specified, nouns take unpossessed form (marked with suffix)
   - Same suffix used as nominalizer on verbs
   - Alienably possessed nouns: possessive prefix + additional suffix (based on noun meaning)

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Head-marking**: Grammatical relations marked on head (verb), not dependents
- **Masculine/feminine distinction**: Gender must be tracked for proper agreement
- **Classifier systems**: Correct classifier selection requires semantic knowledge of noun properties
- **Possession alienability**: Different constructions for alienable vs. inalienable possession

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Arawakan languages
- ResearchGate: "Arawak Languages"
- Oxford Research Encyclopedia: "Morphology in Arawak Languages"
- ScienceDirect: "Some morphological elements of Maipuran Arawakan"
- SIL.org: "A Grammar Sketch and Lexicon of Arawak (Lokono Dian)"

### 7. Sino-Tibetan (18 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Burmese, Chin (Eastern Khumi, Matu, Siyin, Thado, Thaiphum, Zyphe), Chinese (Mandarin), Limbu, Sunwar, Tamang (Eastern), Zaiwa

**Geographic distribution**: East Asia, Southeast Asia, South Asia (China, Myanmar, India, Nepal, Bhutan)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Tonal Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Most languages possess phonemic tones
   - Tone distribution varies considerably:
     - Tibetan: 2 tones
     - Burmese: 3 tones
     - Mandarin Chinese: 4 tones
     - Cantonese: up to 9 tones
   - Classical Tibetan was NOT tonal
   - Modern varieties (Central, Khams Tibetan) developed tone registers
   - Proto-Sino-Tibetan: can reconstruct conditioning factors for tones, NOT tones themselves

2. **Classifier Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Noun is typically collective term (all members of class)
   - Counted/modified by demonstrative only indirectly through classifiers
   - Classifiers = smaller number of non-collective nouns
   - Phenomenon ABSENT in Tibetan
   - Appears late in Burmese and Chinese
   - NOT exclusively Sino-Tibetan: also in Hmong-Mien, Tai, Austric, Japanese

3. **Structural Characteristics** (Source: WikiFreedom)
   - Vast majority of words monosyllabic
   - Word order: object-verb (except Chinese, Bai, Karenic, Mruic which are SVO/VSO)
   - Analytic grammar: relies heavily on word order rather than inflection

4. **Morphological Diversity** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Structurally one of world's most diverse families
   - Gradation from isolating to polysynthetic:
     - Isolating: Lolo-Burmese, Tujia
     - Polysynthetic: Gyalrongic, Kiranti

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Tone critical for meaning**: Tonal errors change word meanings entirely
- **Classifier selection**: Requires semantic knowledge of noun categories
- **Analytic structure**: Grammatical relations conveyed by word order, not morphology
- **Monosyllabic tendency**: Complex ideas expressed through multi-word compounds
- **Word order variation**: Some branches verb-final, others verb-medial

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Sino-Tibetan languages, Tibetic languages
- Britannica: "Sino-Tibetan languages - Tonal, Isolating, Synthetic"
- Britannica: "Sino-Tibetan languages - Noun Classifiers, Grammar, Morphology"
- MustGo.com: "The Sino-Tibetan Language Family"

### 8. Quechuan (18 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Inga, Quechua (Cajamarca, Eastern Apurímac, Huallaga, Huamalíes-Dos de Mayo Huánuco, Huaylas Ancash, Huaylla Wanca, Lambayeque, Margos-Yarowilca-Lauricocha, North Bolivian, North Junín, Northern Conchucos Ancash, Panao, San Martín, South Bolivian, Southern Conchucos), Quichua (Northern Pastaza, Southern Pastaza)

**Geographic distribution**: South America (Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia, Argentina, Chile)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Agglutination** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Very highly agglutinating
   - Words built from basic roots + multiple suffixes
   - Each suffix carries one meaning
   - Very regular (not isolating or fusional)
   - Single words can express complex ideas (sometimes lengthy)

2. **Evidentiality** (Source: Wikipedia - most distinctive feature)
   - THREE-TERM EVIDENTIAL SYSTEM
   - Three morphemes mark varying levels of source information
   - Speakers must indicate source of information:
     - Direct evidence (saw it)
     - Reported evidence (heard it)
     - Inferred evidence (inferred it)
   - NOT grammatical requirement in most European languages
   - Evidentials are "second position enclitics"
   - Usually attach to first constituent in sentence

3. **Bipersonal Conjugation** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Verbs agree with BOTH subject and object

4. **Additional Grammatical Features** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Topic particles
   - Benefactive suffixes (who benefits from action)
   - Attitudinal suffixes (speaker's attitude toward action)

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Evidentiality obligatory**: Translators must interpret/add evidential information absent in source languages without evidentiality
- **Agglutinative structure**: Long words with multiple suffixes require careful morpheme-by-morpheme analysis
- **Bipersonal agreement**: Both subject and object marked on verb
- **Topic marking**: Information structure differs from subject-prominent languages
- **Attitude marking**: Speaker perspective grammatically encoded

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Quechuan languages, Southern Quechua
- Cambridge Handbook: "A Typological Overview of Aymaran and Quechuan Language Structure"
- Oxford Research Encyclopedia: "Morphology in Quechuan Languages"
- Alchetron: "Quechuan languages"

### 9. Creole (15 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Aukan, Belize English Creole, Chavacano, Haitian Creole, Kriol, Lesser Antillean French Creole, Malay (Kupang), Saramaccan, Sea Island English Creole, Sranan Tongo, Tetun Dili, Tok Pisin, Torres Strait Creole

**Geographic distribution**: Global (Caribbean, Pacific, Southeast Asia, South America)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Substrate and Superstrate Influence** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Substrate = native speakers' source language
   - Superstrate = target language speakers compelled to adopt
   - Atlantic Creoles: superstrate = European, substrate = African
   - Lexical features mainly from Western European lexifiers
   - Structural features transferred from West African substrate languages

2. **Selective Feature Transmission** (Source: Nature.com)
   - Creoles reproduce typological features of substrate languages
   - Transmission is SELECTIVE:
     - Bound morphology: NOT transferred (too complex)
     - Tonal structures: NOT transferred (paradigmatic complexity)
     - Morphological and tonal systems: simplified in language contact

3. **Word Order** (Source: Britannica)
   - SVO word order most common in pidgins and creoles

4. **TMA Marking** (Source: Academia.edu)
   - Majority express grammatical categories via three preverbal free morphemes
   - Order: Tense - Mood - Aspect

5. **Simplification Patterns** (Source: Nature.com)
   - Complex paradigms simplified
   - Bound morphology replaced with free morphemes
   - Analytic structures preferred over synthetic

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Mixed ancestry**: Must understand both substrate and superstrate influences
- **Simplified morphology**: Less inflectional complexity than source languages
- **Free morphemes**: Grammatical relations expressed by separate words, not affixes
- **Substrate syntax**: Often preserves word order patterns from substrate
- **Bible translation**: Tok Pisin has Bible translation, indicating creoles are fully developed for religious texts

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Creole language
- ResearchGate: "Creole language structure between substrates and superstrates"
- Nature.com: "Not all grammatical features are robustly transmitted during the emergence of creoles"
- Britannica: "Substratum language"

### 10. Tucanoan (14 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Barasana-Eduria, Carapana, Cubeo, Macuna, Paicoca, Piratapuyo, Siona, Siriano, Tanimuca-Letuama, Tatuyo, Tucano, Tuyuca, Waimaha, Wanano

**Geographic distribution**: Northwest Amazon (Colombia, Brazil, Peru, Ecuador - Vaupés region)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Noun Classification** (Source: Oxford Bibliographies)
   - Extensive noun classification systems
   - Animate/inanimate nouns have clear morphological opposition
   - Masculine/feminine gender categories exist

2. **Evidentiality** (Source: Wikipedia - Tuyuca)
   - Particularly well-developed in family
   - Tuyuca has FIVE evidentiality paradigms:
     - Visual
     - Nonvisual
     - Apparent
     - Second-hand (past tense only)
     - Assumed (not in 1st person present)
   - Evidential affixes also express:
     - Tense (past/present, not future)
     - Person
     - Number
     - Gender

3. **Agglutination** (Source: Cysouw manuscript)
   - Grammatical meanings expressed by agglutinated suffixes

4. **Language Similarity** (Source: Cysouw)
   - High degree of cognate correspondences
   - Grammars quite similar across family

5. **Areal Diffusion** (Source: WALS)
   - Evidentiality spread through contact
   - Neighboring non-Tucanoan languages adopted similar systems (Vaupés region)

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Evidential precision**: Five-way evidential distinction requires careful interpretation
- **Gender marking**: Agreement throughout system
- **Evidential-tense fusion**: Evidentiality interacts with tense marking
- **Contact effects**: Evidentiality may appear in neighboring languages through borrowing
- **Translation complexity**: Non-specialists rely on English translations + additional explanations

**Sources consulted**:
- Oxford Bibliographies: "Tucanoan Languages"
- Wikipedia: Tuyuca language
- Cysouw.de: "Tucanoan" manuscript
- WALS Online: "Chapter Semantic Distinctions of Evidentiality"

### 11. Sepik (14 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Abau, Alamblak, Ambulas, Hanga Hundi, Iatmul, Iwam (Sepik), Kwanga, Kwoma, Manambu, Mende, Saniyo-Hiyewe, Yessan-Mayo

**Geographic distribution**: Papua New Guinea (Sepik River basin and adjoining areas)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Simple Phonology** (Source: Britannica)
   - Few consonants or vowels
   - Usually no tones
   - Appear to have three-vowel systems: /ɨ ə a/
   - Distinguish only vowel height
   - High preponderance of central vowels

2. **Unusual Vowel Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Many Papuan languages (especially Sepik-Ramu basin) have unusual vowel systems
   - Very high use of central vowels

3. **Noun Class Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Torricelli, Sepik, Lower Sepik-Ramu have noun class systems
   - Up to 10 noun classes
   - Each class has specific singular/plural suffixes
   - Related adjectives and verbs show concord

4. **Complex Verb Morphology** (Source: Academia.edu)
   - Rich phonological systems
   - Diverse grammatical structures
   - Complex verb morphology
   - Example from Alamblak: mi-brɲi-r = "he went down"
     - Literally: "down + go away + he"

5. **Classification Status** (Source: Academia-lab)
   - Sepik-Ramu hypothesis controversial
   - Features like distinctive pronouns may be language area features, not genetic

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Simple phonology**: Fewer phonemic distinctions than many languages
- **Noun classes**: Correct class assignment affects agreement throughout sentence
- **Verb complexity**: Directional and aspectual meanings incorporated into verb
- **Central vowels**: May be difficult to perceive/transcribe for speakers of languages lacking central vowels

**Sources consulted**:
- Britannica: "Papuan languages - Phonemes, Dialects, Morphology"
- Wikipedia: Papuan languages, Sepik languages
- Academia.edu: "The Papuan Languages of New Guinea"
- Academia-lab: "Sepik-Ramu languages"

### 12. Tupian (12 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Guajajára, Guaraní (Eastern Bolivian, Mbyá, Western Bolivian), Guarayu, Kaapor, Kaiwá, Kayabí, Mundurukú, Sateré-Mawé, Sirionó, Tenharim

**Geographic distribution**: South America (Amazon basin - original homeland between Mamoré and Aripuanã rivers in Madeira River Basin)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Rich Morphology** (Source: Oxford Research Encyclopedia)
   - Expressed mainly by suffixes and prefixes
   - Particles also important for grammatical categories
   - Verbal morphology characterized by rich valence-changing formations

2. **Word Order** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Basic word order in most families: SOV
   - Change under European language contact

3. **Noun Incorporation** (Source: Wikipedia - Old Tupi)
   - Objects of transitive verbs may come before, after, or incorporated into verb
   - When incorporated:
     - 1st class verbs: after person markers
     - Otherwise: before root

4. **Word Formation Processes** (Source: ACL Anthology)
   - Diminutive/augmentative
   - Compounding
   - Reduplication
   - Noun incorporation

5. **Proto-Tupí Diversification** (Source: Oxford Research Encyclopedia)
   - Started diversifying around 5,000 years ago
   - Homeland: Mamoré-Aripuanã confluence area

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **SOV word order**: Different from Portuguese/Spanish contact languages
- **Incorporation**: Single verb word can include noun argument
- **Valence operations**: Rich system of causatives, applicatives affecting argument structure
- **Low-resource context**: TuLaR project works on computational/pedagogical materials for Brazilian indigenous languages

**Sources consulted**:

- Oxford Research Encyclopedia: "Tupian Languages"

### 13. Torricelli (12 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Au, Bukiyip, Kamasau, Kombio, Mufian, Olo, Urat, Urim, Weri

**Geographic distribution**: Papua New Guinea (northern region)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **SVO Word Order** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - UNUSUAL among Papuan languages
   - Basic clause order: Subject-Verb-Object
   - Most Papuan languages have SOV

2. **Noun Inflection for Number** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Nouns inflected for number
   - NOT generally found in:
     - Trans-New Guinea
     - Sepik
     - Lakes Plain
     - West Papuan
     - Alor-Pantar
     - Tor-Kwerba families

3. **Complex Noun Class Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Upwards of 10 noun classes
   - Most classes assigned phonologically (based on final segment of stem)
   - Nouns carry complex inflections marking:
     - Number distinctions
     - Noun classes

4. **Grammatical Gender** (Source: De Gruyter)
   - Gender likely marked at root of Torricelli languages
   - Pronouns and verbs at core of system

5. **Pronoun Reconstruction** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Independent personal pronouns:
     - *ki = "I"
     - *(y)i = "thou"
     - *(y)ip = "you (pl)"
   - Second-person pronouns = strong diagnostics for Torricelli membership

6. **Unusual Features** (Source: ResearchGate - Walman)
   - Two words function as noun phrase conjunctions
   - Have morphology of transitive verbs
   - Exhibit:
     - Subject agreement with first conjunct
     - Object agreement with second conjunct

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **SVO order**: Unlike most Papuan languages, matches English/Romance order
- **Noun classes**: Class assignment affects agreement patterns
- **Number marking**: Obligatory number distinction on nouns
- **Unusual conjunctions**: "And" expressed by verb-like elements with agreement

### 21. Nilo-Saharan (8 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Bedjond, Dholuo, Dinka (Southwestern), Kenga, Morokodo, Sabaot, Shatt, Uduk

**Geographic distribution**: Central/East Africa (Sudan, South Sudan, Kenya, Uganda, Chad)

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Tone Systems** (Source: Britannica)
   - Most languages tonal (2-4 tones)
   - Some peripheral varieties lack tones (contact with non-tonal Afro-Asiatic)
   - Western Lugbara: up to 4 tonal levels

2. **Verb Morphology** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Extensive marking for person, number, tense, aspect, voice
   - Consonant mutation often accompanies morphological processes
   - Widespread perfective/imperfective aspect distinction
   - Common verbal features:
     - 1st person singular: prefix a-
     - 2nd person singular: prefix i-
   - Person/number marked by verbal prefixes

3. **Singulative Marking** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Singular marked, plural is basic unmarked form
   - Collective nouns marked in singular to single out one item

4. **Nominal Case** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Case marking widespread but absent in areas contiguous to Niger-Congo
   - Cases marked by suffixes or tonal differences

5. **Vowel Harmony** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Prominent in Central Sudanic, Nilotic, Surmic groups
   - Restricts co-occurrence of vowels from more than one harmonic set

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Tonal variation**: Tone may mark case or other grammatical categories
- **Aspect-oriented**: Perfective/imperfective more important than tense
- **Vowel harmony**: Harmonic sets must be respected in affixation
- **Case systems**: Variable across family

**Sources consulted**:

### 24. Turkic (6 languages)

**Languages in dataset**: Azerbaijani (South), Turkish, Uyghur

**Geographic distribution**: Central Asia, Eastern Europe, Western Asia, Siberia

**Unique grammatical features**:

1. **Vowel Harmony** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Almost universal within family
   - Inherited from Proto-Turkic (already fully developed)
   - Words consist of syllables with either back or front tongue position
   - Most suffixes vary according to preceding syllable (back or front sounds)
   - Develops due to muscular economy and harmonic sound production
   - Range: Tuvan = almost fully harmonic, Uzbek = least harmonic

2. **Agglutination** (Source: Britannica)
   - Extensive agglutination via suffixes and postpositions
   - Rich possibilities for expanding stems
   - Relatively unchangeable and clear-cut suffixes
   - Many designate grammatical notions
   - Words formed by adding suffixes to root/stem
   - Complex ideas conveyed with single word

3. **Null-Subject Languages** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Subject can be omitted
   - Lack grammatical articles
   - Lack noun classes
   - Lack grammatical gender

4. **Word Order** (Source: Wikipedia)
   - Subject-Object-Verb universal within family

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Vowel harmony**: Suffix vowels must harmonize with stem
- **Agglutination**: Long words with multiple suffixes
- **SOV order**: Verb-final structure
- **No gender**: Unlike many Indo-European languages

**Sources consulted**:
- Wikipedia: Turkic languages, Turkish grammar, Vowel harmony
- Britannica: "Turkic languages - Altaic, Agglutinative, Turkic-speaking"
- FluentinTurkish.com: "Turkish Vowel Harmony: Rules and Examples"

### 26. Totonacan (7 languages)

   - Extensive use of prefixes and suffixes

   - Free combination of applicatives with each other and with causative

   - Special prefixing combining forms of body parts added to verbs
   - Delimit verb's locus of affect
   - Indicate which part of subject/object affected by action

4. **Lack of Case and Prepositions** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - No morphological case on nouns
   - Many languages lack prepositions
   - Rich system of:
     - Causatives
     - Applicatives
     - Body part prefixes

5. **Mesoamerican Areal Features** (Source: ResearchGate)
   - Preference for verb-initial order
   - Head-marking
   - Extensive use of body part morphemes in metaphorical/locative constructions

**Translation-relevant distinctions**:

- **Applicative stacking**: Multiple applicatives increase verb valency
- **Body part prefixes**: Grammaticalized body part meanings
- **5-argument verbs**: Can have very complex argument structures
- **No prepositions**: Spatial/grammatical relations in verb morphology
