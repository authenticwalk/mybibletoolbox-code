# 1 Corinthians 1:23 - Exclusive Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference

**1 Corinthians 1:23** - Epistle genre (Doctrinal teaching)

**Context**: Paul writing to the church at Corinth. Paul uses "we" to refer to himself and the apostles/preachers, explicitly contrasted with the recipients (Corinthians) and the audiences being addressed (Jews and Greeks/Gentiles).

**English (eng-LSV)**:
> "also **we** preach Christ crucified, to Jews, indeed, a stumbling-block, and to Greeks foolishness,"

**Greek source (grc-macula)**:
> ἡμεῖς δὲ κηρύσσομεν Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον, Ἰουδαίοις μὲν σκάνδαλον, ἔθνεσιν δὲ μωρίαν

> Key pronouns: ἡμεῖς (hēmeis - "we"), κηρύσσομεν (kēryssomen - "we preach")

**Morphological Analysis**:
- **ἡμεῖς** (hēmeis): first person plural nominative pronoun
  - Person: First
  - Number: Plural
  - Discourse referent: n46001013013 n46001001010 (Paul and co-workers/apostles)
- **κηρύσσομεν** (kēryssomen): first person plural present active indicative verb
  - Morphology: V-PAI-1P (Present Active Indicative, 1st Person Plural)

**Participants**:
- **Speaker group**: Paul + apostles/preachers ("we" - EXCLUSIVE)
- **Addressee**: Corinthian church (letter recipients - outside the speaker group)
- **Message audiences mentioned**: Jews and Greeks (also outside the speaker group)
- **Clear grammatical separation**: The "we" refers to those who preach, excluding both the Corinthian recipients and the audiences being preached to

## Key Insight

This verse demonstrates **EXCLUSIVE clusivity** with clarity. The speaker group (Paul + apostles/fellow preachers) is grammatically separated from the addressee (Corinthian church). The context is epistolary: Paul is writing TO the Corinthians ABOUT what "we" (Paul and his co-workers) preach. The Corinthians are the recipients of the letter, not members of the preaching group, making this a clear example of exclusive first person plural.

## Linguistic Significance

### Why This Verse Clearly Demonstrates Exclusive Clusivity

1. **Epistolary genre**: Letter structure inherently separates writer ("we") from recipients ("you")
2. **Participant roles**:
   - Preachers (Paul + apostles): Active speakers ("we preach")
   - Corinthians (recipients): Addressees (being taught)
   - Jews/Greeks: Message audiences (those being preached to)
3. **Discourse context**: Paul contrasts different groups' responses to the message
4. **No ambiguity**: Unlike some verses where "we" could theoretically be inclusive (e.g., exhortations), this epistolary context makes exclusion clear

### Translation Implications

For translators working in clusivity-marking languages:
1. **Must use EXCLUSIVE forms** - epistolary context requires it
2. **Clear models**: Indonesian, Tagalog, Tok Pisin, Hawaiian provide excellent templates
3. **Theological accuracy**: EXCLUSIVE correctly reflects Paul (+ apostles) vs Corinthian recipients
4. **Avoid error**: Using INCLUSIVE would grammatically include the Corinthians in the preaching group, contradicting the letter's structure

## Recommendations for Bible Translation

### For Clusivity-Marking Languages

**REQUIRED**: Use EXCLUSIVE first person plural forms throughout this verse.

**Examples to follow**:
- Tagalog: "kami/namin/aming" (NOT "tayo/natin/atin")
- Indonesian: "kami" (NOT "kita")
- Tok Pisin: "mipela" (NOT "yumi")
- Hawaiian: "makou" (NOT "kakou")

### For Non-Clusivity Languages

**Recommendation**: Ensure epistolary context is clear:
- Maintain distinction between writer and recipients
- Consider using pronoun emphasis if grammatically appropriate ("we ourselves," "we [apostles]")
- Ensure translation preserves the letter genre's inherent writer-recipient separation

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. ❌ Using INCLUSIVE forms in clusivity languages (would include recipients in preaching group)
2. ❌ Obscuring the epistolary context (writer to recipients)
3. ❌ Failing to distinguish between preacher group and recipient group
4. ❌ Translating "we" ambiguously where grammar permits clarity
