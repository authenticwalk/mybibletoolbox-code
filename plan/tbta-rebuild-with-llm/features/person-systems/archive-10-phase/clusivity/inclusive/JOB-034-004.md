# Job 34:4 - INCLUSIVE Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference and Context

**Reference**: Job 34:4
**Genre**: Wisdom Literature - Dialogue
**English (eng-LSV)**: "Let us choose judgment for ourselves, || Let us know among ourselves what [is] good." {eng-LSV}

**Why This Verse**: Job 34:4 exemplifies first-person inclusive plural in a wisdom dialogue context. Elihu, the younger speaker, addresses Job and his three friends, inviting them to join together in discernment. The verse contains two parallel "us" constructions ("let us choose... let us know"), both clearly including both the speaker (Elihu) AND the addressees (Job and friends) in the activity of discernment. This makes it a paradigmatic example of INCLUSIVE clusivity.

**Context**: This verse occurs in Elihu's first speech (Job 33-34), where he positions himself as a mediator between Job and the three older friends. By using inclusive language, he creates solidarity with his audience while establishing his role as fellow-seeker of truth rather than superior judge.

## Discourse Context Analysis

### Speaker and Addressees

**Speaker**: Elihu (the younger friend)
**Addressees**: Job + Eliphaz + Bildad + Zophar (4 people)
**Discourse function**: Elihu is establishing common ground, inviting his audience to join him in collaborative truth-seeking

### Why This Must Be INCLUSIVE

1. **Semantic content**: "Let us choose... let us learn" - the verbs describe joint activity
2. **Context**: Elihu is addressing his listeners, inviting them into the process
3. **Pragmatics**: If Elihu meant exclusive ("we judges, not you Job"), he would be excluding the very people he's addressing, which is pragmatically incoherent {llm-cs45}
4. **Rhetorical strategy**: Inclusive language creates solidarity and reduces confrontation - appropriate for a younger speaker addressing elders {llm-cs45}
5. **Parallel with Job 34:2-3**: Elihu just said "Hear my words, you wise men" (v.2) and "the ear tests words" (v.3) - verse 4 continues this by inviting them to join the testing process {llm-cs45}

## Theological and Translation Implications

### 1. Wisdom Literature Discourse Patterns

Job 34:4 exemplifies a common pattern in wisdom literature where speakers invite their audience into collaborative reasoning:
- "Let us reason together" (Isaiah 1:18) {llm-cs45}
- "Come, let us go up to the mountain" (Isaiah 2:3) {llm-cs45}
- "Let us consider how to stir up one another" (Hebrews 10:24) {llm-cs45}

**Pattern**: Wisdom teachers use INCLUSIVE language to create dialogical space rather than monological pronouncement {llm-cs45}.

### 2. Translation Guidance for Clusivity Languages

**For translators working in languages that mark clusivity** (Austronesian, some Papuan, some Amerindian languages):

✅ **USE INCLUSIVE forms** for Job 34:4:
- Tagalog: tayo, natin, atin (NOT kami, namin, amin)
- Indonesian: kita (NOT kami)
- Cebuano: kita, nato, atong (NOT kami, namo, amo)
- Ilocano: -tayo suffix (NOT -kami)
- Tok Pisin: yumi (NOT mipela)
- Fijian: kedaru/keimami (inclusive 3+ persons) vs. keirau/kemami (exclusive) {llm-cs45, unconfirmed}

❌ **AVOID EXCLUSIVE forms** - would incorrectly imply Elihu is excluding his addressees from the discernment process

### 3. Rhetorical Function of Inclusive Language

Elihu's use of inclusive language serves multiple rhetorical functions:

1. **Solidarity building**: Creates common ground with his audience {llm-cs45}
2. **Authority mitigation**: As a younger speaker, inclusive language reduces perceived presumption {llm-cs45}
3. **Collaborative epistemology**: Presents truth-seeking as joint effort, not individual revelation {llm-cs45}
4. **Invitation to dialogue**: Opens space for response rather than demanding submission {llm-cs45}

**Implication for translation**: Preserving the inclusive clusivity (in languages that mark it) preserves these important rhetorical and relational functions {llm-cs45}.

### 4. Hebrew Source Text

**Hebrew**: משפט נבחרה־לנו נדעה בינינו מה־טוב
- "mishpat nivkharah-lanu ned'ah beinenu mah-tov"
- "judgment let-us-choose-for-us let-us-know among-us what-good"

**Grammar**:
- נבחרה (nivkharah) = 1st person plural cohortative "let us choose"
- נדעה (ned'ah) = 1st person plural cohortative "let us know"
- לנו (lanu) = "for us"
- בינינו (beinenu) = "among us / between us"

**Clusivity in Hebrew**: Biblical Hebrew does NOT grammatically mark inclusive vs. exclusive {llm-cs45}. The forms are simply first-person plural. However, the discourse context (Elihu addressing his listeners) and the prepositional phrases ("for us", "among us") strongly indicate inclusive reading {llm-cs45}.
