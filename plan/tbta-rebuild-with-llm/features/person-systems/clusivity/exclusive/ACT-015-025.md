# Acts 15:25 - Exclusive Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference

**Acts 15:25** - Epistle within Narrative genre

**Context**: The apostles and elders in Jerusalem writing a letter to Gentile believers in Antioch, Syria, and Cilicia. The verse is part of the official letter from the Jerusalem Council, where the leadership group ("we" = apostles and elders) addresses the church recipients ("you" = Gentile believers). The "we" explicitly excludes the recipients, making this a clear case of exclusive clusivity.

**English (eng-LSV)**:
> "it seemed good to us, having come together with one accord, to send to you chosen men, with **our** beloved Barnabas and Paul—"

**Greek source (grc-SR)**:
> ἔδοξεν ἡμῖν γενομένοις ὁμοθυμαδόν, ⸂ἐκλεξαμένους⸃ ἄνδρας πέμψαι πρὸς ὑμᾶς, σὺν τοῖς ἀγαπητοῖς ἡμῶν Βαρνάβᾳ καὶ Παύλῳ,
>
> Key pronouns:
> - ἡμῖν (hēmin - "to us", dative first person plural)
> - ἡμῶν (hēmōn - "our", genitive first person plural)
> - ὑμᾶς (hymas - "you", accusative second person plural)

**Participants**:
- **Speaker group**: Apostles and elders in Jerusalem ("we" - EXCLUSIVE)
- **Addressee**: Gentile believers in Antioch, Syria, and Cilicia ("you" - outside the speaker group)
- **Clear grammatical separation**: The "we" (letter writers) explicitly excludes "you" (letter recipients)

## Key Insight

This verse demonstrates **EXCLUSIVE clusivity** with exceptional clarity. The apostolic leadership group writing the letter uses "we" to refer to themselves, contrasted with "you" (the recipients). The phrase "our beloved Barnabas and Paul" shows that even these two apostles are being grouped with the Jerusalem leadership ("our"), excluding the recipients. This makes it impossible for "we/us/our" to include the addressees - making this a prime example of exclusive first person plural in epistolary context.

## Linguistic Significance

### Why This Verse is Clear for Exclusive Clusivity

1. **Epistolary genre**: Letters inherently create sender/recipient distinction
2. **Official ecclesiastical context**: Apostolic leadership writing to churches
3. **Explicit contrast**: "we decided... to send to you" - impossible to include recipients in decision-makers
4. **Participant roles**:
   - Sender group: Active (deciding, choosing, sending)
   - Recipient group: Passive (receiving messengers and letter)
5. **Historical context**: Acts 15 Jerusalem Council - leadership makes decision for broader church
6. **No ambiguity**: Unlike some verses where "we" could theoretically include addressee, this epistolary context makes exclusion explicit

### Translation Implications

For translators working in clusivity-marking languages:
1. **Must use EXCLUSIVE forms** - epistolary context demands it
2. **Clear models**: Tagalog, Indonesian, Tok Pisin, Hawaiian, Ilokano, Tongan provide excellent templates
3. **Theological accuracy**: EXCLUSIVE correctly reflects apostolic authority structure
4. **Avoid error**: Using INCLUSIVE would grammatically include recipients in the decision-making process, which contradicts the hierarchical structure and historical situation
