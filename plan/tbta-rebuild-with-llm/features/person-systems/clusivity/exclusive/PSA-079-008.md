# Psalm 79:8 - EXCLUSIVE Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference and Context

**Reference**: Psalm 79:8
**Genre**: Poetry - Lament Psalm {ebible}
**English (eng-ULB)**: "Do not hold the sins of our forefathers against us; may your merciful actions come to us, for we are very low." {eng-ULB}

**Why This Verse**: Psalm 79:8 is a paradigmatic example of first-person EXCLUSIVE plural in Scripture. The psalmist is praying TO God, and "us/our" explicitly refers to the human community (Israel) EXCLUDING the addressee (God). This creates a clear speaker-addressee distinction: God is the "you" being addressed, and the people are the "us/we" making the petition. This makes it an ideal test case for exclusive clusivity analysis.

**Theological Context**: This is part of a communal lament after the destruction of Jerusalem. The people plead for God's mercy, asking Him not to punish them for their ancestors' sins. The exclusive nature is theologically important: "us" = sinful humanity in need, "you" (God) = the merciful one being petitioned.

## Theological and Translation Implications

The unanimous use of EXCLUSIVE forms in clusivity-marking languages provides linguistic support for the Creator-creature distinction {llm-cs45}:

### 2. Translation Guidance for Clusivity Languages

**For translators working in languages that mark clusivity** (Austronesian, some Papuan, some Amerindian languages):

✅ **USE EXCLUSIVE forms** for Psalm 79:8 and similar prayer contexts:
- Tagalog: kami, namin, amin (NOT tayo, natin, atin)

- Cebuano: kami, namo, amo (NOT kita, nato, atong)

- Tongan: kimautolu, emau (NOT inclusive forms)
- Hawaiian: makou (NOT kakou)

❌ **AVOID INCLUSIVE forms** - would theologically imply:
- God is part of the sinful petitioning group
- God shares in human guilt and weakness
- Blurs Creator-creature boundary

**General Rule for Prayer Contexts**:
- Prayers TO God: Use EXCLUSIVE (we = petitioners, excluding God)
- God speaking about Godhead: Use INCLUSIVE (we = within Trinity, as in Gen 1:26)
- Exhortation to worship: Check context (may be inclusive if speaker includes addressees in "we")

### 3. Languages Without Clusivity Marking

**For translators in languages WITHOUT clusivity marking** (English, Spanish, French, German, most Indo-European, most African, etc.):

- Use standard plural forms ("we/us/our")
- Maintain clear pronominal distinction between first person (we) and second person (you/your)
- Clusivity distinction must be addressed in **study notes** or **commentary**, not in translation text
- Cannot mark inclusive/exclusive grammatically - language doesn't allow it

**Study Note Example**:
> "In languages that distinguish inclusive and exclusive first-person plural, this verse uses exclusive forms: 'we' refers to the human petitioners and explicitly excludes God, the one being addressed."
