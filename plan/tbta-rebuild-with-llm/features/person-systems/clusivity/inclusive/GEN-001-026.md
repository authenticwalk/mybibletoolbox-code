# Genesis 1:26 - INCLUSIVE Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference and Context

**Reference**: Genesis 1:26
**Genre**: Climactic Narrative Story {GEN-001-026-tbta.yaml}
**English (eng-LSV)**: "And God says, 'Let Us make man in Our image, according to Our likeness...'" {eng-LSV}

**Why This Verse**: Genesis 1:26 is the paradigmatic example of first-person inclusive plural in Scripture. God speaks using plural pronouns ("us", "our"), and the theological question of WHO is included in this "us" makes this a critical test case for clusivity analysis. This verse occurs at the climax of the creation narrative, before the creation of humanity.

## Theological and Translation Implications

### 1. Trinitarian Interpretation Support

The unanimous use of INCLUSIVE forms in clusivity-marking languages provides linguistic support for the Trinitarian interpretation of Genesis 1:26 {llm-cs45}:

- **Inclusive = internal divine council**: God addresses other members of the Godhead
- **If exclusive were used**: Would suggest God addressing beings outside the Godhead (angels, humans not yet created)
- **Result**: Austronesian translations inadvertently provide grammatical evidence for the Trinity debate

### 2. Translation Guidance for Clusivity Languages

**For translators working in languages that mark clusivity** (Austronesian, some Papuan, some Amerindian languages):

‚úÖ **USE INCLUSIVE forms** for Genesis 1:26:
- Tagalog: tayo, natin, atin (NOT kami, namin, amin)
- Indonesian: kita (NOT kami)
- Cebuano: kita, nato, atong (NOT kami, namo, amo)
- Fijian: kedaru/keimami (inclusive 3+ persons) vs. keirau/kemami (exclusive) {llm-cs45, unconfirmed}

‚ùå **AVOID EXCLUSIVE forms** - would theologically imply God + beings outside Godhead

### 3. Languages Without Clusivity Marking

**For translators in languages WITHOUT clusivity marking** (English, Spanish, French, German, most Indo-European, most African, etc.):

- Use standard plural forms ("we/us/our")
- Clusivity distinction must be addressed in **study notes** or **commentary**, not in translation text
- Cannot mark inclusive/exclusive grammatically - language doesn't allow it

### 4. Ambiguity in Source Text

**Hebrew Source**: Biblical Hebrew does NOT grammatically mark inclusive vs. exclusive {llm-cs45}

- The form ◊†÷∑◊¢÷≤◊©÷∂◊Ç◊î (na'aseh) "let us make" is simply 1st person plural cohortative

  - Cross-reference with other passages

**Implication**: The unanimous inclusive choice by Austronesian translators represents a theological decision consistent with mainstream Christian interpretation, not a requirement from the Hebrew grammar itself {llm-cs45}.
