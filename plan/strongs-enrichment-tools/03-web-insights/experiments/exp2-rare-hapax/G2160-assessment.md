## Challenges & Limitations

### Content Synthesis Challenge

**Problem:** Sources focus on virtue ethics (positive sense of eutrapelia), while Ephesians 5:4 uses it negatively.

**Solution Strategy:** Frame as "Paul's cautionary use of a classical virtue"
- Explain positive meaning (classical context)
- Show Paul's inversion of meaning (why he chose this word to condemn)
- Connect to boundaries for Christian recreation
- Avoid confusion: "This isn't about banning humor; it's about choosing virtuous humor"

### Audience Alignment Challenge

**Problem:** Most sources written for Catholic/Reformed theological audiences, not general Bible study users.

**Solution:** Translate theological language to accessible framework
- Replace "temperance virtue" with "healthy balance in recreation"
- Explain "golden mean" in contemporary terms
- Connect Aquinas to practical Christian life, not just scholastic theory

### Limited Exegetical Coverage

**Problem:** Classical commentaries mention the word but don't provide extensive analysis of Ephesians 5:4 context.

**Solution:** Combine exegetical with theological sources
- Use Jamieson, Fausset & Brown for Ephesian cultural context (city known for wit)
- Use theological sources for virtue framework
- Create integrated explanation: culture + virtue + prohibition

### Single Occurrence Risk

**Problem:** Hapax legomenon might not justify extensive content; readers won't encounter word frequently.

**Solution:** Frame value as broader principle, not just word definition
- Title: "Eutrapelia: When Classical Virtue Becomes Christian Excess"
- Focus: Understanding Paul's caution about humor, applicable to all recreation discussions
- Utility: Relevant to broader conversation about Christian speech and amusement
