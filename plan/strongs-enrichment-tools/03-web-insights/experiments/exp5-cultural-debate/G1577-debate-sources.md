## Sources by Position

### Position 1: "Assembly" or "Congregation" Strongly Preferred

#### Source 1.1: Cooper Abrams (bible-truth.org)

**Author:** Dr. Cooper P. Abrams, III
**Credentials:** Doctorate holder (limited biographical detail provided)
**URL:** https://www.bible-truth.org/Ekklesia.html

**Position Statement:**
Ekklesia should be translated as "assembly" or "congregation," not "church." The current translation enables false doctrines about universal churches and hierarchical governance.

**Main Arguments:**
1. **Etymology:** Greek term originally meant "a called out assembly of citizens" with geographical specificity
2. **Contextual use:** NT instances always require modifiers identifying specific local gatherings
3. **Linguistic error:** English "church" derives from unrelated Greek "kyriakon" (Lord's house), creating semantic confusion
4. **Theological implications:** Universal church concepts lack biblical basis and contradict local assembly autonomy

**Historical Claim:**
Traces mistranslation to Theodore Beza (1556) to support Presbyterian hierarchical governance, with subsequent English Bibles maintaining this choice for ecclesiastical conformity.

**Acknowledgment of Other Views:** Minimal to none
**Tone:** **Dogmatic** - Uses phrases like "false doctrine" and "heretical teaching"; presents position as objective linguistic fact rather than interpretive argument

**Authority Level:** Self-published; doctoral credentials but no institutional affiliation mentioned

#### Source 1.3: Historical Precedent - William Tyndale

**Historical Context:**
- Tyndale (1494-1536) chose "assembly" and "congregation" to translate ekklēsia
- KJV translators received explicit instruction: "The Old Ecclesiastical Words to be kept, viz. the Word Church not to be translated Congregation"
- Some sources suggest Tyndale's translation choices contributed to persecution

**Significance:**
Demonstrates translation choice has been contentious since Reformation; institutional interests influenced standardization

## Patterns Across Sources

### Areas of Agreement

1. **Etymology:** All sources agree on basic etymology: ek ("out of") + kaleo ("to call")
2. **Classical usage:** Political/civic assembly in Greek culture
3. **Septuagint usage:** Renders Hebrew qahal (assembly of Israel)
4. **NT range:** Refers primarily to gathered believers
5. **Historical controversy:** Translation choice contentious since Reformation (Tyndale vs KJV)
6. **Semantic drift:** Meaning shifted over time (by 200 AD, included buildings)

### Areas of Divergence

1. **Relevance of etymology:**
   - Some emphasize "called out ones" remains theologically significant
   - Others argue etymology less important than contextual usage

2. **Institutional baggage:**
   - Critics: "church" carries problematic hierarchical/building associations
   - Defenders: Modern "church" can accurately convey original meaning if properly understood

3. **Universal vs local:**
   - Some argue universal church concept requires "church" over "assembly"
   - Others contend universal church concept itself is mistranslation artifact

4. **Translation philosophy:**
   - Formal equivalence leans toward "assembly" (closer to Greek semantic range)
   - Dynamic equivalence may retain "church" (familiar term with right connotations when explained)

### Stakes of Translation Choice

**Theological:**
- Ecclesiology: local autonomy vs denominational structure
- Polity: congregational vs hierarchical governance
- Universality: invisible/universal church vs local gatherings only

**Cultural:**
- Post-colonial contexts: "church" associated with colonialism
- House church movements: prefer "assembly" to avoid institutionalism
- Traditional denominations: "church" carries cherished heritage

**Linguistic:**
- Building vs people: "church" risks confusion about physical structures
- Familiarity: "church" universally recognized; "assembly" requires explanation

**Pastoral:**
- Community emphasis: "called out ones" highlights identity
- Gathering emphasis: "assembly" highlights regular meeting
- Institutional reality: many contexts have established "churches"

## Key Findings for Tool 3

### 1. Ongoing Legitimate Debate

This is not a settled issue where one view is "correct." Multiple scholars and traditions hold different positions with substantive arguments.

### 2. Translation Influenced by Theological Assumptions

Historical evidence (KJV translator instructions, Tyndale's persecution) shows translation choices have been driven by ecclesiological commitments, not just linguistics.

### 3. Both Translations Capture Aspects of Meaning

- "Church" captures: community identity, covenantal continuity, specialized religious gathering
- "Assembly" captures: political/civic overtones, local gathering emphasis, avoids building confusion

### 4. Context Matters for Translation Choice

- **Secular assembly (Acts 19:39, 41):** "assembly" clearly correct
- **Religious gathering:** both "church" and "assembly/congregation" defensible
- **Universal body:** "church" may be preferred; "assembly" can seem inadequate

### 5. Cultural Sensitivity Required

Translators must consider:
- Does target culture associate "church" with colonialism?
- Is there institutional baggage in target language equivalents?
- What theological tradition will use the translation?

### 6. Tone Varies Widely

- **Dogmatic sources:** Present one view as objectively correct (often "assembly" advocates)
- **Traditional sources:** Defend "church" without acknowledging debate
- **Balanced sources:** Acknowledge complexity, present trade-offs

### 7. Scholarly Consensus: Complexity Acknowledged

Higher-authority sources (SBL, academic exchanges) tend to acknowledge translation complexity rather than declare single answer.
