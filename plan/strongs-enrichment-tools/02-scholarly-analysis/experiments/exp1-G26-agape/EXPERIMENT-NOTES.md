## Research Strategy and Process

### Phase 1: Initial Source Discovery (30 minutes)

**Approach:**
- Web searches for peer-reviewed articles using terms: "agape", "JBL", "New Testament Studies", "Catholic Biblical Quarterly"
- Searches for major scholarly works: Spicq, Nygren, Furnish, Outka
- Lexicon consultation: BDAG, TDNT, LSJ, Louw-Nida references

**Challenges:**
- Web search tool had intermittent availability (some searches returned "unavailable")
- Open-access peer-reviewed articles difficult to find; relied on one strong JBL article plus standard lexicons
- Google Scholar access limited without institutional login

**Solutions:**
- Focused on well-known scholarly monographs and commentaries (Tier 2)
- Used standard lexicons (BDAG, TDNT, LSJ) as Tier 1 foundation
- Leveraged one excellent JBL article (Shepherd 2010) for peer-reviewed component

### Phase 2: Theological Significance & Debates (45 minutes)

**Research Focus:**
- Agapē vs philē debate (semantic distinction or stylistic variation?)
- Agapē vs eros dichotomy (Nygren's thesis and critiques)
- Whether agapē is uniquely biblical or found in classical Greek

**Key Findings:**
- **Debate 1 (agapē/phileō):** Emerging consensus toward semantic overlap with theological preference for agapē vocabulary
- **Debate 2 (agapē/eros):** Nygren's dichotomy influential but methodologically flawed; scholarly critique well-documented
- **Debate 3 (classical usage):** Evidence from LSJ and Robert Joly shows broader classical usage than traditionally assumed

**Sources Used:**
- Shepherd (JBL 2010): Primary source for agapē/phileō debate
- Nygren (1930): Agapē/eros dichotomy
- Outka (1972): Critique of Nygren
- Barrett, Carson: Supporting arguments for interchangeability
- LSJ, BDAG: Classical Greek usage data

### Phase 3: Cultural Context & Diachronic Analysis (40 minutes)

**Research Focus:**
- 1st century cultural context
- Classical Greek usage (Homer, Xenophon, Aesop, etc.)
- Hellenistic/LXX usage
- Koine/NT development
- Patristic usage

**Key Findings:**
- Classical Greek: Present but uncommon (verb more common than noun)
- LXX: Chosen to translate Hebrew אָהַב (aheb) across full semantic range
- NT: Paul makes it "virtually a technical term"; phileō falling out of use by late 1st century
- Patristic: Fully established as Christian love term (though Nygren claims synthesis with eros)

**Sources Used:**
- LSJ: Classical usage documentation
- Spicq (3 vols): Comprehensive diachronic treatment
- BDAG: Notes on classical rarity and LXX prominence
- Web searches: LXX translation patterns, Septuagint usage

### Phase 4: Intertextual Connections (25 minutes)

**Research Focus:**
- OT/Hebrew equivalents (אָהַב vs חֶסֶד)
- LXX translation patterns
- Greco-Roman literature parallels
- Dead Sea Scrolls (limited - Hebrew/Aramaic texts)

**Key Findings:**
- LXX uses agapaō/agapē for Hebrew אָהַב (aheb), never for חֶסֶד (chesed)
- LXX usage is broader than later NT theological specification
- Classical parallels (Plato's eros, Xenophon's distinctions) help contextualize NT choices
- DSS provide background for understanding distinctiveness of Jesus's "love enemies" teaching

**Sources Used:**
- LXX translation data from web searches and lexicons
- Nygren: Discussion of Platonic eros vs biblical agapē
- Xenophon references from classical sources

### Phase 5: Translation Guidance & Misconceptions (20 minutes)

**Research Focus:**
- Translation challenges (English "love" covers all Greek terms)
- Common misconceptions (rigid agapē/phileō distinction)
- Popular vs scholarly understanding

**Key Findings:**
- Major misconception: "Agapē = divine unconditional love; phileō = human friendship" (not supported by lexicography)
- Etymological fallacy: Assuming agapē inherently means self-sacrifice (meaning developed through usage, not etymology)
- Translation challenge: English lacks separate words for love types

**Sources Used:**
- Carson, Shepherd, Barrett: Refuting rigid distinctions
- Furnish: On translation and interpretation challenges
- BDAG: Etymology vs usage

**Challenge:** Difficult to find open-access full-text peer-reviewed journal articles on agapē.
**Impact:** Moderate - limited Tier 1 journal article sources to 1 primary article (Shepherd JBL 2010)

### 2. **Web Search Tool Availability**
**Challenge:** Some web searches returned "unavailable" error during research session.
**Impact:** Minor - required multiple search attempts and alternative query formulations
**Mitigation:** Persisted with different searches; focused on well-known scholarly works that could be located through multiple search strategies

### 3. **Vast Literature on Agapē**
**Challenge:** Agapē is one of the most extensively studied NT Greek words; overwhelming amount of scholarly literature available.
**Impact:** Positive challenge - selecting best sources rather than finding any sources
**Mitigation:** Prioritized premier scholars (Spicq, Brown, Fee) and classic works (Nygren) plus contemporary peer-reviewed (Shepherd)

### 4. **Balancing Debate Representation**
**Challenge:** Fairly representing all sides of scholarly debates (especially agapē/phileō where popular teaching differs from scholarship).
**Impact:** Required careful weighing of evidence and sources
**Mitigation:** Included strong voices on both sides; used synthesis sections to acknowledge complexity; avoided oversimplification

### 5. **Classical Greek Evidence**
**Challenge:** Evidence for classical Greek usage scattered across various sources; LSJ lexicon not fully accessible online.
**Impact:** Moderate - required synthesis from multiple secondary sources
**Mitigation:** Used multiple sources (LSJ references in web searches, BDAG notes on classical usage, Nygren's discussion, Joly's work) to triangulate data

### 6. **Avoiding Over-Citation of Same Source**
**Challenge:** Spicq's 3-volume work on agapē is definitive but can't be only source cited.
**Impact:** Required diverse sourcing to avoid over-reliance
**Mitigation:** Balanced Spicq with Brown (Johannine), Fee (Pauline), Furnish (ethics), Outka (philosophy), Shepherd (linguistics)

## Key Learnings for Methodology

### What Worked Well

1. **Balanced Sourcing Strategy:** Combining Tier 1 lexicons + Tier 2 monographs + 1 excellent peer-reviewed article provided comprehensive coverage
2. **Structured Research Phases:** Breaking research into phases (significance → debates → culture → diachronic → intertextual → translation) ensured comprehensive coverage
3. **Real-Time Citation:** Adding sources to ATTRIBUTION.md during research prevented orphaned citations
4. **Synthesis Discipline:** Using {llm-cs45} only for synthesis prevented LLM-generated theology

### What to Adjust

1. **Front-Load Journal Searches:** Search for peer-reviewed articles earlier before settling on monographs
2. **Time Management:** Agapē took 180 minutes (upper end); consider 120-150 minutes for less central theological words
3. **Statistical Verification:** Create quick reference for Greek NT statistics (total occurrences, distribution) to cite properly
