## Word Overview: G5100 τις

### Basic Data
- **Lemma:** τις (neuter: τι)
- **Transliteration:** tis
- **Gloss:** someone, anyone, a certain one; something, anything
- **Part of Speech:** Enclitic indefinite pronoun
- **Frequency:** 526 occurrences (mGNT), 542 (TR)

### Key Distinction
**Enclitic Indefinite τις vs Interrogative τίς:**
- **τις (no accent):** "someone, anyone, something" - indefinite
- **τίς (acute accent):** "who? what?" - interrogative
- This distinction was the **core pedagogical focus** of the extraction

**Implication:** Cycle 4 grammatical pathway scales consistently across frequency ranges for pronouns.

---

### Finding 2: Enclitic Distinction is Pedagogically Rich ✅

**Focus:** Interrogative τίς (acute accent) vs indefinite τις (no accent)

**Implementation:**
- Etymology: Clearly explained enclitic nature
- Functional categories: Separated indefinite functions
- Pedagogical insights: Challenge #1 addresses distinction
- Throughout: Consistently emphasized

**Result:** Pedagogical richness score 1.0/1.0 despite being grammatical pronoun

**Insight:** Accent/enclitic distinctions provide excellent pedagogical value when clearly explained - not just "technical grammar" but meaningful for readers.

---

### Finding 3: Conditional Usage Drives Epistolary Frequency ✅

**Pattern Discovered:**
- Narrative genres (Luke-Acts): Higher raw frequency for indefinite noun modification ("a certain priest")
- Epistolary genres (1 Cor, Romans): High conditional usage ("if anyone...")

**Insight:** Genre distribution analysis (Cycle 4 refinement) reveals functional specialization - pronouns serve different rhetorical purposes in different genres.

**Pedagogical Value:** Helps translators anticipate usage patterns by genre.

---

### Finding 4: Nominative Dominance Pattern (Differs from G846) ✅

**G846 αὐτός:** 94% oblique cases (genitive/dative/accusative dominant)
**G5100 τις:** 54% nominative (nominative heavily dominant)

**Why:**
- αὐτός: Third-person pronoun primarily in oblique reference
- τις: Indefinite subject marker primarily in nominative ("someone says")

**Insight:** Morphological distribution patterns are word-specific, not just "pronoun patterns" - each pronoun has unique case distribution reflecting functional specialization.
