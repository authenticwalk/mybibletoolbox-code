### 2. Controversies Documented (6 major debates)

**Full controversy detection for ultra-critical theological term:**

**A. Pronunciation: Yahweh vs Jehovah**
- **Scholarly consensus:** Yahweh (based on Greek transliterations: Ιαουε, Ιαβε)
- **Jehovah origins:** Introduced c. 1520 by Galatinus, combining YHWH consonants with Adonai vowels
- **Scholarly rejection:** Smith's Bible Dictionary (1869), RSV (1952), modern lexicons reject as hybrid
- **Alternative reconstructions:** Romer (Yahô/Yahû), Reisel (YeHūàH/YaHūàH)
- **Conclusion:** Original pronunciation not preserved; Yahweh most likely reconstruction

**B. Translation: LORD vs Yahweh in Bible Versions**
- **LORD position (majority):** KJV, NIV, ESV, NASB follow LXX κύριος precedent; practical clarity
- **Yahweh position (scholarly):** Jerusalem Bible, Legacy Standard Bible; scholarly accuracy
- **Jehovah position (sectarian):** ASV 1901, New World Translation; rejected by mainstream scholars
- **Vatican 2008 directive:** Prohibits "YHWH" in liturgy, requires "Lord" or "God"
- **Debate continues:** Balancing accuracy, tradition, theology, and readability

**C. Etymology: Exodus 3:14 Explanation**
- **Traditional view:** YHWH derives from hayah (H1961 "to be"), explained in Exodus 3:14 "I am who I am"
- **Critical challenge:** Some scholars argue Exodus 3:14 is "popular etymology" not original meaning
- **Grammatical objection:** "He is" would use yod as third letter, not vav
- **Alternative proposals:** May mean "to cause to become," "to come to pass," various theories
- **Consensus:** While debated, traditional interpretation remains theologically significant and lexically defended

**D. Pronunciation Prohibition: Why and When**
- **Traditional Jewish reasons:** Reverence (Exodus 20:7), blasphemy prevention, magical misuse, profane oaths
- **Philo's explanation:** "Only those purified unto wisdom may hear or speak" the divine name
- **Result:** Adonai substituted; original pronunciation lost
- **Christian debate:** Should Christians restore pronunciation or honor Jewish/apostolic tradition?
- **Sectarian responses:** Sacred Name Movement, Jehovah's Witnesses emphasize restoration

**E. Christological Connections: YHWH-Kyrios-Jesus**
- **NT pattern:** Quotes OT YHWH passages, applies to Jesus using kyrios
- **Key passages:** Philippians 2:9-11 (Isaiah 45:23), Romans 10:13 (Joel 2:32), Hebrews 1:10 (Psalm 102)
- **Trinitarian implications:** NT identification of Jesus with YHWH supports deity of Christ
- **Sectarian responses:** Jehovah's Witnesses argue for sharp distinction
- **Mainstream Christianity:** Sees identification as intended, supporting high Christology

**F. Documentary Hypothesis: Source-Critical Questions**
- **Pattern:** Elohist source avoids YHWH in Genesis, uses after Exodus 3 revelation
- **J vs E sources:** Jahwist uses YHWH from Genesis 2; Elohist uses Elohim until Exodus 3
- **Conservative response:** Literary artistry and theological purpose, not necessarily source division
- **Current scholarship:** Focus shifted to theological message in canonical form

**Assessment:** Exceptional controversy coverage - 6 substantive debates with multiple positions, evidence, scholarly consensus documented. Covers linguistic, translational, theological, and critical issues.

### 3. Diachronic Development (7 stages, 2,000+ year span)

**Stage 4: Judges/Monarchy (1375-930 BCE)**
- More compound names (Shalom - Judges 6:24)
- Theophoric personal names common (Jonathan, Jehoshaphat)
- Temple centralization (Solomon's temple, 1 Kings 8)

**Stage 5: Divided Kingdom/Prophets (930-586 BCE)**
- Highest usage period: Jeremiah (555x), Isaiah (significant)
- Prophetic formula: "Thus says YHWH"
- Compound names: Tsidkenu (Jeremiah 23:6), Adonai YHWH frequent
- Monotheism emphasis: YHWH sole God, not merely patron deity

**Stage 6: Exilic/Post-exilic (586 BCE onwards)**
- Pronunciation prohibition develops (reverence, exile concerns)
- Books without YHWH: Esther, Song, Ecclesiastes (specific contexts)
- Psalms peak usage (645x) - liturgical/worship vehicle
- Shammah (Ezekiel 48:35) - "The LORD is there"

**Stage 7: Second Temple/LXX (3rd-1st century BCE)**
- Septuagint translates as κύριος (kyrios, "Lord")
- Exodus 3:14 rendered: "I am the one who is" (ontological emphasis)
- Jewish custom solidified: HaShem ("the Name") designation
- Philo documents reverence tradition
- Establishes precedent for Christian "Lord" translation
