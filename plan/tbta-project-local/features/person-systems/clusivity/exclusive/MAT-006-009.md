# Matthew 6:9 - EXCLUSIVE Clusivity Analysis

## Verse Reference

**Matthew 6:9** - "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name"

**Genre**: Gospel (Teaching/Instruction)

**Literary Context**: Jesus teaching his disciples the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:5-15)

## Why This Verse Demonstrates EXCLUSIVE Clusivity

This verse is a **paradigmatic example** of exclusive clusivity because:

1. **Speaker**: Jesus (addressing his disciples)
2. **Audience**: The disciples (and by extension, all believers)
3. **Addressee of prayer**: God the Father
4. **"Our" referent**: Jesus + disciples + all believers (speaker + audience)
5. **"Our" excludes**: God the Father (the addressee of the prayer)

This creates the classic EXCLUSIVE scenario: "our" includes the speaker and audience but explicitly excludes the addressee (God).

## Source Language Analysis

### Greek (Koine)

**Text**: Πάτερ ἡμῶν ὁ ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς {grc-SR}

**Key word**: **ἡμῶν** (hēmōn) - first person plural genitive pronoun "our/of us"

**Grammatical analysis**:
- ἡμῶν is the genitive form of ἡμεῖς (hēmeis, "we")
- First person plural
- Genitive case (possessive): "of us" / "our"
- Koine Greek does **not grammatically encode** clusivity distinction {llm-cs45}
- The exclusive interpretation comes from **pragmatic context**: the addressee (God) cannot be included in "our Father" since He is the Father being addressed

**Morphology from Macula** {macula-greek}:
- The verse begins with: οὕτως οὖν προσεύχεσθε ὑμεῖς ("Thus therefore pray you")
- ὑμεῖς (hymeis) - second person plural "you" (the disciples) {MAT-006-000-macula}
- The prayer that follows is what "you" (disciples) should pray

## Phase 5: Theological and Linguistic Insights

### Why This Verse Matters for Clusivity

**Paradigmatic status**:
This verse serves as a **prototypical example** of exclusive clusivity in:
1. **Prayer contexts**: Address to deity creates natural exclusive scenario
2. **Teaching discourse**: Jesus (speaker) teaches disciples (audience) about relating to God (addressee)
3. **Clear boundaries**: Sharp distinction between "we who pray" vs "God who is prayed to"

**Contrast with inclusive contexts**:
- Genesis 1:26 "Let us make" - Trinity speaking → INCLUSIVE (among divine persons)
- John 11:15 "Let us go" - Jesus + disciples going together → could be INCLUSIVE
- Matthew 6:9 - Believers addressing God → EXCLUSIVE (God excluded from "our")

---

### Pragmatic Force

**Why EXCLUSIVE is obligatory here**:

1. **Vocative structure**: "Father" is direct address (vocative)
   - The addressed party cannot be included in "our"
   - Logical/pragmatic constraint

2. **Prayer genre**: Prayer inherently creates speaker/addressee separation
   - Prayer = believers speaking TO God
   - "Our Father" = Father OF believers
   - God is Father, not co-pray-er

3. **Possessive semantics**: "Our Father" = "Father who belongs to us"
   - "Us" = those doing the praying
   - God is the possession, not the possessor

**Typological note** {llm-cs45}:
This pragmatic pattern is likely **universal** across languages with clusivity. It would be typologically exceptional for any language to use inclusive here.

---

### Textual Boundaries and Scope

**Verse structure**:
```
[Introduction] "Therefore pray like this:"
[Prayer begins] "Our Father in heaven..."
```

**Clusivity applies to**:
- "Our" in "Our Father" → EXCLUSIVE
- Continued throughout Lord's Prayer:
  - "Give **us** this day our daily bread" → EXCLUSIVE
  - "Forgive **us** our debts" → EXCLUSIVE
  - "Lead **us** not into temptation" → EXCLUSIVE

**Consistent referent**: "us/our" throughout the prayer = believers (excluding God who is addressee)
